## Post 1
- Date: 2026-04-10 (6 days ago)
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/brendanhufford_last-year-i-rebuilt-the-newsletter-function-activity-7447991844979793920-lIxF

Last year, I rebuilt the newsletter function for 6 B2B SaaS companies. By breaking every "rule" the newsletter gurus swear by:

- Nobody tracked clicks
- Nobody A/B tested a subject line
- Not a single "We're excited to announce..." in sight
- No "here's our latest 3 blogs + a webinar"

My only rule: whatever the number of emails you think you need to write - delete half of them.

Most B2B companies think to send newsletters regularly, they have to write them regularly. Wrong.

Recycling 50% of our newsletter content INCREASED engagement (and cut our workload in half). It's called the "Immortal Newsletter" strategy:

- 50% is timeless, high-performing content on rotation
- 50% is fresh, trending insights

Results:
- Zero subscriber complaints about "repeat" content
- 2x faster to produce
- Higher read rates than previous "100% fresh" approach

Most subscribers don't read every single newsletter. So they're actually missing your best content. Get off the newsletter hamster wheel. It was always optional.

## Post 2
- Date: 2026-04-09 (1 week ago)
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/brendanhufford_in-84-general-motors-blew-45-billion-on-activity-7447635124361478145-JWUz

In '84, General Motors blew $45 billion on the "Factory of the Future." Almost every SaaS is making the same mistake with AI.

40 years ago, GM tried to replace workers with robots — placed exactly where human workers had been. Same assembly line. Same pace. Same sequence. Just machines instead of people. The robots sometimes painted each other instead of cars. Manufacturing costs went up, not down. Total disaster.

Toyota had access to the same robots but asked a different question: what becomes possible now that wasn't possible before? Plant layouts got redesigned. Work cells reorganized. Quality feedback became immediate.

This is exactly what's happening with AI and content right now. Most companies are doing the GM thing: "We used to have a writer do this. Now ChatGPT does it." Same briefs. Same approval process. Same distribution. Faster mediocrity is still mediocrity.

The teams actually winning with AI aren't asking "how do we produce more content?" They're asking "why were we producing this content in the first place?"

If you're using AI to do the same corporate, copycat, commodity content faster, you're GM in 1984. If you're using AI to rethink what should exist at all, you're Toyota.
